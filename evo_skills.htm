How I Have Grown as a Software Developer and as a Person

Kyle Kinsella | Jan 10th 2026

| | |

Introduction

On this page, I share how I have grown both as a software developer/engineer and as a person. This growth has reshaped my perspective on life and fundamentally changed how I approach and solve problems.


Why Am I Writing This?

The main reason I am writing this is to reflect on how my mindset has changed over time.

When I was younger, I often had a very rigid approach to decisions - if I did not want to do something, I simply would not do it. For example, if I did not want to sleep, I would stay awake. There was no consideration of consequences or long-term impact.

As I matured and became an adult, this mindset changed significantly. Using the same example today, instead of refusing to sleep, I would stop and consider why sleep is important: resting the brain, recharging the body and preparing myself for the next day.

While this may seem like a simple or even odd example, it closely mirrors the reality of software development/engineering. Much of the work involves pausing, thinking ahead and considering consequences before acting.

For instance:

  • Old Kyle: I don’t want to write this unit test.
  • New Kyle: I don’t want to write this unit test, but what happens if this code breaks the system? If that happens, I am responsible. Writing the test now prevents a much bigger problem later.
In simple terms, this page demonstrates how I have grown from Old Kyle into New Kyle, both professionally and personally.


Old Kyle

If younger Kyle did not want to do something, then it simply did not get done. There was no reflection, no planning and no consideration of long-term outcomes. The decision was final.


New Kyle

Story 1: Learning to Drive

At one point, my mindset was: If I don’t learn to drive, so what?

But then I took a step back and thought about the reality of my situation. Living in a remote area, how would I realistically get to Dublin? How would this impact my independence and my family?

Yes, learning to drive requires time, effort and commitment. However, once you begin and stay consistent, everything starts to fall into place. By changing my mindset, I was able to see the long-term benefits rather than focusing only on the immediate inconvenience.

Story 2: Facing Complexity in Software Development

In 2025, while still working toward landing my first software developer role, I reached a point of frustration. I decided I needed a challenging project to focus on and grow from. After some research, I found a book titled “Writing an Interpreter in Go.” It sounded interesting and seemed like a solid way to deepen my understanding, so I bought it.

When the book arrived, I was excited to begin. However, once I opened it, I was quickly confronted with the reality of its complexity. The material was very low-level and far more challenging than I had anticipated.

Old Kyle’s approach: I jumped straight into coding. After about an hour, I was completely lost. I did not fully understand the concepts or the code, yet I continued pushing forward regardless. After several hours, frustration turned into anger. I was confused, overwhelmed and visibly irritated, simply because I did not understand what I was building. That was Old Kyle.

New Kyle’s approach:

The following day, I returned to the project with a fresh mind and a new perspective. I made a clear decision: I will not continue until I truly understand what is happening.

This moment felt like a switch flipping in my brain.

Since then, I have applied this approach to every project, task and challenge I face. I prioritize understanding over speed, clarity over brute force and reflection over frustration. Without question, this has been the most effective and sustainable way for me to grow as a developer.


Summary

Instead of immediately saying, No, I’m not doing that, I now do the opposite:

  • Step back
  • Pause
  • Take a break (if needed)
  • Return after a few minutes
  • If you are angry or confused, stop
  • If possible, leave the project for a day or two
  • Only then return to the task with a clear and focused mindset
If you follow this approach, you may be surprised by the positive impact it has. Not only on your workflow, but also on your overall mindset and personal growth.
